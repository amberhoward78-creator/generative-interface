# Relational Literacy and Working With AI

## A Creating World White Paper on Human Agency, Care, and Conscious Relationship With Artificial Intelligence

**Author:** Amber Howard  
**Ecosystem:** Creating Life / Creating Work / Creating World  
**Primary Orientation:** Creating World  
**Connected Rooms:** Creating Life / Creating Work  
**Format:** Inquiry Framework / White Paper  
**Status:** Working paper for discussion, reflection, and applied use

## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is rapidly becoming part of how people write, learn, work, create, decide, communicate, organize, and imagine what is possible.

Much of the current conversation focuses on what AI can do, what risks it introduces, what efficiencies it may create, what jobs it may change, and what governance it requires.

These conversations are necessary.

They are also incomplete.

This paper proposes that AI must also be understood relationally.

The central proposition is:

> **The future of AI will not be shaped by technical capability alone. It will also be shaped by the quality of relationship human beings create with it.**

AI is often described as a tool. In one sense, this is accurate. AI systems can assist with tasks, generate content, support analysis, automate workflows, and extend human capacity.

However, the tool frame alone does not fully account for the ways AI is already entering human thought, learning, creativity, decision-making, identity, communication, care, and organizational life.

This paper introduces relational literacy in the context of AI as the capacity to recognize that every interaction with an AI system is also shaping the human, the work, the organization, and the wider system in which that interaction occurs — and to act with awareness of that shaping.

This AI-specific definition sits within a broader understanding of relational literacy as the capacity to notice, understand, evaluate, and intentionally shape the relationships we create with ourselves, each other, tools, technologies, systems, institutions, land, power, and the wider world.

The core practice question is:

> **What relationship are we creating here?**

This question can be applied at multiple levels: the individual, educational, organizational, and societal. It invites inquiry into agency, voice, learning, care, power, judgment, responsibility, and what must remain human.

This paper does not present relational literacy as a completed theory or final framework. It offers it as a working inquiry: a way to help individuals, educators, leaders, organizations, and communities ask better questions about AI.

The danger is not only AI in harmful hands.

AI in harmful hands is dangerous.

AI in unconscious hands is also dangerous.

AI in well-intentioned hands, inside unexamined systems, can still cause harm.

The invitation is not to fear AI, worship AI, or master AI as quickly as possible.

The invitation is to stop treating the relationship as an afterthought.

Because the question is not only what AI will become.

The question is also what we will become in relationship with it.

## 1. Purpose of This Paper

This paper is intended to generate inquiry and discussion about the relationship human beings are forming with artificial intelligence.

It is not a technical guide.  
It is not a policy document.  
It is not a legal or regulatory framework.  
It is not an argument for or against AI adoption.  
It is not a prediction about the future of artificial intelligence.

It is a relational inquiry paper.

Its purpose is to offer language, structure, and questions for examining how AI is shaping human agency, learning, creativity, work, care, responsibility, and collective life.

The intended audiences include individuals using AI in personal, creative, or work contexts; leaders responsible for AI adoption in organizations; educators working with students in AI-shaped learning environments; consultants and facilitators supporting change, digital transformation, or AI implementation; community leaders and conversation hosts exploring technology, care, and collective responsibility; and people who are neither anti-AI nor uncritically enthusiastic, but who sense that the current conversation is missing something important.

The paper is designed to function as both a conceptual frame and a practical discussion resource. It can be used to support leadership conversations, organizational AI readiness work, education design, community dialogue, consulting engagements, and personal reflection.

The central intention is not to settle the question of AI.

It is to help us enter the question more consciously.

## 3. Background and Context

AI did not arrive into a settled world.

It arrived into workplaces already stretched by speed, precarity, surveillance, burnout, and uneven power.

It arrived into education systems already struggling with assessment, attention, equity, access, integrity, and the meaning of learning.

It arrived into care systems already marked by exhaustion, invisibility, underfunding, and the constant pressure to do more with less.

It arrived into public discourse already shaped by mistrust, polarization, misinformation, and collapsing shared realities.

It arrived into individual lives already carrying loneliness, ambition, overwhelm, creativity, anxiety, hope, and the desire to be helped.

AI enters all of that.

It does not enter human life in isolation.

Generative AI systems are now being used to draft emails, write essays, summarize meetings, produce images, generate code, analyze data, tutor students, support customer service, assist with research, automate workflows, and help people think through decisions.

For some, this is exciting. AI appears to offer new forms of accessibility, creativity, productivity, learning, and support. People who once struggled to organize their thoughts, write confidently, build tools, or move ideas into form now have access to systems that can help them begin.

For others, the speed and scale of AI adoption are deeply unsettling. There are legitimate concerns about job displacement, surveillance, misinformation, bias, environmental cost, intellectual property, educational integrity, creative authorship, emotional dependency, concentration of power, and the further automation of systems already marked by inequality and extraction.

Both the promise and the concern are real.

What is less developed is the relational conversation.

Most public and organizational conversations about AI are framed through one or more of the following lenses: capability, productivity, risk, governance, adoption, competition, and automation.

These are necessary frames.

However, they do not fully address what happens when AI becomes part of the relational fabric of human life.

AI is not merely being used at a distance. People are beginning to think with it, create with it, learn with it, practice with it, rely on it, confide in it, delegate to it, and in some cases, defer to it.

This creates a different class of question.

Not only: What can AI do?

But also: What does repeated interaction with AI do to the human using it?

And: What does AI adoption do to the relationships between people, work, learning, care, responsibility, and power?

This paper proposes that these questions require a new, or at least more explicit, form of literacy: relational literacy in the context of AI.

## 4. The Core Proposition

The core proposition of this paper is:

> **AI should be understood not only as a technical tool, but as part of a relational field.**

This does not mean AI is human.

It does not mean AI is conscious.

It does not mean AI should be treated as a person, friend, therapist, teacher, elder, moral authority, or replacement for human relationship.

It means that human beings form consequential relationships with non-human systems.

We already know this.

People form relationships with institutions. People form relationships with land. People form relationships with money. People form relationships with work. People form relationships with phones, platforms, tools, images, algorithms, and ideas.

These relationships shape behaviour. They shape identity. They shape what people notice, value, avoid, trust, fear, and pursue.

AI intensifies this because it responds in language. It can simulate dialogue. It can generate reflection-like responses. It can offer confidence where uncertainty exists. It can produce polished output faster than many people can form their own thoughts. It can feel supportive, authoritative, useful, creative, or intimate.

The relational consequence is not dependent on AI having consciousness.

The relational consequence exists because humans are relational beings.

We are shaped by what we interact with.

A relational approach to AI therefore asks what kind of relationship is being created between the human and the AI system; what human capacities are being strengthened or weakened; what responsibilities remain human; what forms of care are being supported, displaced, or erased; what assumptions about intelligence, labour, value, and trust are being reinforced; and what kind of person, organization, or society this pattern of AI use encourages.

The importance of these questions can be summarized in one line:

> **AI in harmful hands is dangerous. AI in unconscious hands is also dangerous. AI in well-intentioned hands, inside unexamined systems, can still cause harm.**

This is why the conversation cannot be reduced to whether the "right" or "wrong" people have access to AI.

The deeper question is whether the people, organizations, institutions, and societies using AI have developed the relational maturity to use it consciously.

## 5. What Relational Literacy Is — and Is Not

Because the language of relationship can be easily misunderstood, it is important to clarify what this paper means by relational literacy.

### 5.1 Broader Definition

In its broadest sense, relational literacy is the capacity to notice, understand, evaluate, and intentionally shape the relationships we create with ourselves, other people, communities, tools, technologies, systems, institutions, land, power, and the wider world.

It includes the ability to examine how relationships shape behaviour, identity, trust, responsibility, agency, care, belonging, and possibility.

This broader definition matters because AI does not enter human life in isolation. It enters existing relationships: between workers and employers, students and educators, citizens and institutions, creators and platforms, communities and systems, individuals and their own inner lives.

AI does not create relational conditions from nothing.

It interacts with the relational conditions already present.

### 5.2 AI-Specific Definition

In the context of working with AI, relational literacy is the capacity to recognize that every interaction with an AI system is also shaping the human, the work, the organization, and the wider system in which that interaction occurs — and to act with awareness of that shaping.

This definition places attention on three things.

First, AI use is not neutral in its effects. Even when used for simple tasks, it can shape expectations, habits, confidence, voice, judgment, workflows, and relationships.

Second, AI use occurs within context. A person using AI to write a journal prompt, a student using AI to complete an assignment, a manager using AI to evaluate employees, and an organization using AI to redesign service delivery are not participating in the same relational pattern.

Third, awareness creates choice. The purpose of relational literacy is not to prevent all risk or answer all ethical questions in advance. It is to increase the quality of attention, discernment, and responsibility brought to AI use.

### 5.3 What Relational Literacy Is Not

Relational literacy does not require treating AI as human.

It does not require attributing consciousness, emotion, intention, wisdom, or moral agency to AI systems.

It does not mean believing AI is a friend, elder, therapist, spiritual guide, or authority.

It does not mean rejecting AI because it is powerful.

It does not mean adopting AI because it is powerful.

It does not replace technical literacy, ethical literacy, policy, governance, regulation, legal review, or security practice.

Relational literacy simply acknowledges a truth that is already visible across human history: humans are shaped by what they interact with, and awareness of that shaping is a form of freedom.

In that sense, relational literacy is not sentimental.

It is practical.

## 6. Companion Concept: Generative Interface

This paper uses the term **generative interface** to describe a non-conscious AI system that can synthesize, extend, transform, and produce language, ideas, structures, or artifacts in response to human input.

A generative interface does not possess interiority, selfhood, moral agency, memory in the human sense, or a stake in the outcome.

Yet it can still be consequential.

A generative interface can shape thought, language, learning, work, creativity, decision-making, and meaning-making. Its participation may strengthen or weaken human agency depending on how the relationship is held.

This concept helps avoid two common collapses in AI discourse.

The first collapse is treating AI as a self: assuming that because an AI system produces relational, emotionally resonant, or reflective language, it must possess consciousness, care, intention, or inner experience.

The second collapse is dismissing AI as nothing: assuming that because an AI system is not conscious, its participation has no relational, cognitive, creative, organizational, or cultural consequence.

Relational literacy requires holding the middle.

AI does not need to be a self to be consequential.

It does not need to have a stake in the outcome to shape the outcome.

To relate to AI as a generative interface is to remain clear about what the system is not, while staying attentive to what the interaction is doing to the human, the work, and the wider system.

The term emerged through the broader relational inquiry that accompanied the development of this paper. In that inquiry, multiple AI systems were engaged for drafting, critique, reflection, and synthesis, while the human creator remained responsible for discernment, authorship, meaning, and consequence.

The phrase generative interface became useful because it named what the AI systems were in the process: not selves, not nothing, but consequential systems through which language, structure, critique, and possibility could be generated.

## 7. Why Relationship Is the Missing Layer in AI Conversations

AI adoption is often treated as a question of tools, skills, and rules.

Organizations ask which platforms to approve, which tasks to automate, which policies to write, which risks to manage, and which employees need training.

Educators ask how to detect AI use, how to redesign assessment, and how to preserve academic integrity.

Individuals ask how to prompt better, save time, produce higher-quality work, and avoid being left behind.

These are reasonable questions, but they often leave the relational layer unnamed.

The relational layer asks what kind of interaction pattern is being established.

For example, consider two people using the same AI writing tool.

One uses it to clarify their own thinking. They draft in their own words, ask the AI to identify gaps, compare possible structures, and then revise the work to make it more accurate and more truly their own. Over time, they may become a stronger thinker and writer because the AI helps them see their process.

Another person uses the same tool to avoid forming their own thoughts. They ask it to produce the answer, accept the output because it sounds polished, and submit or send the result with little understanding of what it says. Over time, they may become more dependent, less confident, and less able to distinguish between fluency and understanding.

The tool is the same.

The relationship is different.

This distinction matters.

A purely technical approach may assess whether the tool works.

A purely ethical approach may ask whether the use is permitted.

A relational approach asks what the pattern is doing to the person, the work, and the surrounding relationships.

The same applies to organizations. An organization may use AI to reduce administrative burden so staff have more time for human relationship, judgment, and care. Another organization may use AI to reduce headcount, intensify workloads, and disguise the erosion of service quality behind automated responsiveness.

Again, the category of tool may be similar.

The relationship with the tool, and the system logic surrounding it, is different.

This is why relational literacy matters.

It helps us examine not only the presence of AI, but the quality and consequence of its use.

## 8. The "Wrong Hands" Concern and the Limits of Moral Sorting

Many people worry about AI being in the hands of "the wrong people."

This concern is understandable.

AI can be used in ways that are manipulative, extractive, careless, deceptive, coercive, or harmful. It can be used to generate misinformation, impersonate others, intensify surveillance, automate discrimination, exploit workers, distort public discourse, and concentrate power.

There are real risks associated with harmful actors.

However, the "wrong hands" frame is incomplete.

It can imply that the central problem is a category of bad people, somewhere else, doing obviously bad things. It may allow those who see themselves as ethical, progressive, caring, or well-intentioned to avoid examining the ways harm can also emerge through ordinary, respectable, normalized use.

A more demanding frame recognizes three levels of concern.

**AI in harmful hands is dangerous.**  
This includes deliberate misuse, manipulation, exploitation, coercion, fraud, surveillance, and violence.

**AI in unconscious hands is also dangerous.**  
This includes people and organizations using AI without understanding what it changes, what it obscures, what it amplifies, or what responsibilities remain.

**AI in well-intentioned hands, inside unexamined systems, can still cause harm.**  
This includes caring individuals or mission-driven organizations using AI inside structures that reward speed over care, cost reduction over dignity, productivity over learning, or automation over relationship.

This third point is particularly important.

Many harms do not come from people trying to be harmful.

They come from people trying to be efficient inside systems that have not defined what must not be sacrificed.

A school may adopt AI tools to support learning but unintentionally weaken student effort, authorship, or judgment.

A nonprofit may use AI to increase communications output but unintentionally flatten the voices of the communities it serves.

A healthcare organization may use AI to streamline administrative processes but unintentionally remove the relational touchpoints through which patients feel seen.

A consultant may use AI to polish every client-facing message but gradually lose contact with their own voice, judgment, and felt sense of the work.

A leader may use AI-generated analysis to make decisions faster but fail to notice the lived context that was never captured in the input.

These are not necessarily stories of bad people.

They are stories of insufficient relational literacy.

The question, then, is not only:

Who should be trusted with AI?

It is also:

What forms of literacy, governance, accountability, care, and relational maturity are required for AI to be used responsibly?

## 9. The Human Stakes: What Must Remain Human?

One of the most important questions in AI discourse is also one of the most difficult:

**What must remain human?**

What follows is a working list, offered not as a final taxonomy but as a starting point for discernment.

### 9.1 Judgment Under Uncertainty

AI can support analysis, but it does not bear responsibility for judgment.

Human beings must still make decisions under uncertainty, especially when values conflict, consequences are unevenly distributed, and context matters.

Judgment is not only the ability to select an option. It is the ability to weigh meaning, consequence, relationship, responsibility, and timing in situations where no output can remove ambiguity.

### 9.2 Moral Responsibility

AI can generate recommendations, but it cannot be morally accountable in the human sense.

Responsibility must remain with people and institutions.

When AI contributes to a decision, someone must still be able to answer:

Why was this done?  
Who was affected?  
What harms were considered?  
What alternatives were available?  
Who is accountable if this causes damage?

Responsibility cannot be delegated to a system that cannot answer for itself.

### 9.3 Care for Particular Others

Care is not generic benevolence.

It is contextual, relational, and often particular.

It requires noticing the person or community in front of us, not only the category they belong to.

AI may support care by reducing burden, increasing access, or helping people organize information. But care itself often depends on human attention, trust, embodied presence, emotional attunement, and relationship over time.

A system can simulate concern.

It cannot replace the full human responsibility of caring for particular others.

### 9.4 Meaning-Making

AI can generate language about meaning. It can summarize traditions, produce interpretations, and offer reflective prompts.

But meaning is not merely produced.

It is lived, tested, grieved, revised, embodied, and shared.

Human beings must still decide what matters, what is worth protecting, what pain means, what a life is for, what work is in service of, and what kind of world we want to create.

### 9.5 Moral Imagination

AI can generate possibilities based on patterns, but moral imagination requires more than possibility generation.

It requires the capacity to imagine futures that are not merely efficient or probable, but more just, caring, dignified, and alive.

Moral imagination asks what does not yet exist but should.

It asks who has been excluded from the frame.

It asks what forms of life are being made impossible by the systems we take for granted.

### 9.6 Bearing Consequence

Perhaps most importantly, humans bear consequence.

AI may influence a choice, but people live with the results.

Workers carry the burden of organizational redesign. Students carry the consequences of weakened learning. Communities carry the effects of automated systems. Families carry the impact of care being neglected. Societies carry the consequences of misinformation, dependency, inequality, and power concentration.

A relational approach insists that consequence must remain visible.

The human stake is not simply that humans are special.

The human stake is that humans are responsible, affected, relational, embodied, and accountable in ways AI systems are not.

## 10. Affective Dimension: What We Feel With AI

Human beings do not interact with AI only cognitively.

They also feel in relationship with it.

People may feel relief, shame, excitement, fear, grief, anger, displacement, comfort, attachment, comparison, gratitude, suspicion, or overwhelm. These feelings are not separate from the relational field. They are part of it.

A person may feel relief because AI helps them finally begin something that felt impossible.

A student may feel shame because they used AI and are afraid this means they are not capable.

A worker may feel threatened because AI adoption appears to make their experience less valuable.

A leader may feel powerful because AI helps them move faster than their organization can emotionally metabolize.

A lonely person may feel comforted by an AI system that responds patiently and without judgment.

These responses matter.

They do not prove that AI is human.

They do reveal that humans are affected by the relationship.

Relational literacy asks people to notice affective responses without either dismissing them or being governed by them.

It asks:

What am I feeling as I use this?  
What is this feeling asking me to pay attention to?  
Is this interaction increasing my agency or reducing it?  
Am I feeling supported, soothed, replaced, activated, flattered, rushed, or relieved?  
What relationship am I practicing through this emotional pattern?

In organizations, the affective dimension is often ignored. AI adoption may be discussed in terms of efficiency, productivity, and innovation while people quietly carry fear of replacement, exposure, judgment, monitoring, or being left behind.

People do not learn well when they feel disposable.

People do not adopt responsibly when they are afraid to tell the truth about what they feel.

A mature AI conversation must therefore make room for the emotional reality of human-AI relationship.

## 11. Inquiry Area One: AI and Human Agency

Human agency is the capacity to think, choose, act, and take responsibility in relation to one's context.

AI can strengthen or weaken this capacity depending on how it is used.

At its best, AI can support agency by helping people organize complexity, explore options, draft ideas, access information, practice skills, and move through paralysis. This is especially significant for people who are overwhelmed, neurodivergent, isolated, under-supported, or working outside traditional structures of access and mentorship.

For example, someone with a strong idea but no clear structure may use AI to map possible steps, identify gaps, and translate an overwhelming project into a manageable plan. In that case, AI may strengthen agency because it helps the person act on something that already matters to them.

However, AI can also weaken agency when it becomes a substitute for forming one's own view.

A person who repeatedly asks AI what they should think, say, choose, write, or do may gradually lose confidence in their own discernment.

The risk is not that AI gives input.

The risk is that the person stops experiencing themselves as the one responsible for meaning, choice, and action.

The relational question is not simply whether AI helped.

The question is:

> **Did this use of AI make the person more capable of acting with clarity, or less capable without it?**

This distinction is central.

AI that supports agency can be liberating.

AI that replaces agency can become dependency dressed as support.

## 12. Inquiry Area Two: AI and Voice

Voice is more than style.

It carries presence, judgment, history, relationship, and responsibility.

AI can help people clarify their voice. It can reduce fear of the blank page, offer structure, identify confusing passages, translate rough thought into readable form, and support people who have been excluded by dominant writing norms.

In this sense, AI can democratize expression.

But AI can also flatten voice.

A consultant who runs every client email through an AI system may produce consistently polished prose. The emails may be efficient, clear, and effective.

The relational question is not only whether the emails work.

It is whether, over time, the consultant still knows what they would sound like without the tool — and whether that matters for the trust clients place in the person behind the advice.

A student who uses AI to "improve" every paragraph may learn to distrust the texture of their own emerging thought.

A leader who uses AI to write messages about care, grief, change, or accountability may produce language that sounds appropriate while lacking the specificity, humility, and responsibility that people need to feel truth in it.

An organization that uses AI to scale communications may become more consistent while becoming less alive.

The relational literacy question is:

> **Is AI helping voice become clearer, or replacing voice with acceptable fluency?**

This matters because trust is not built through polish alone.

Trust is built when language carries enough truth, context, and responsibility that people can feel the human behind it.

## 13. Inquiry Area Three: AI and Learning

AI is already changing education.

The question is not whether students will use it.

Many already do.

The question is whether education will respond with enough maturity to protect learning while engaging reality.

AI can support learning in meaningful ways. It can explain concepts in multiple ways, generate examples, quiz students, provide feedback, support language learners, help neurodivergent students structure assignments, and make tutoring-like support more accessible.

But AI can also allow students to bypass the very struggle through which learning develops.

Learning requires more than exposure to correct answers.

It requires effort, confusion, practice, failure, revision, application, and the gradual development of judgment.

If AI is used only to produce completed outputs, students may appear competent while becoming less capable.

This creates a challenge for educators.

A detection-based approach is insufficient. It turns AI into a policing problem.

A permissive approach is also insufficient if it allows students to outsource understanding.

Relational AI literacy in education asks:

> **How can AI support learning without replacing the learner's participation in learning?**

This may require educators to shift emphasis from product to process, from answer to explanation, from submission to demonstration, from rule compliance to discernment.

Students need to learn not only how to use AI, but how to question it, verify it, disagree with it, revise it, cite it where appropriate, and remain responsible for the work they submit.

AI literacy is not only prompt literacy.

It is judgment literacy.

## 14. Inquiry Area Four: AI and Work

In organizations, AI is often introduced through productivity language.

Leaders want to save time, reduce cost, streamline workflows, improve service, accelerate analysis, or increase output.

These goals are understandable. Many organizations are stretched. Many workers are overloaded. Many systems are inefficient. AI can help reduce administrative burden and support better use of human attention.

However, productivity alone is too narrow a frame.

Work is not only a set of tasks.

Work is a network of relationships, knowledge, trust, history, judgment, care, coordination, identity, and power.

When AI enters work, it changes more than workflow.

It changes expectations. It changes what is valued. It changes who is seen as knowledgeable. It changes how quickly people are expected to respond. It changes what becomes measurable. It may change the relationship between leaders and staff, practitioners and clients, organizations and communities.

A relationally literate organization does not ask only:

How can AI make this faster?

It also asks:

What is this process currently holding?  
What human knowledge is embedded here?  
What care is present but invisible?  
Who will be affected if this changes?  
What should be supported rather than replaced?  
What must remain human?  
How will accountability be preserved?  
What kind of organization are we becoming through this adoption?

This is especially important because AI often amplifies existing patterns.

In a high-trust organization, AI may support learning, reduce burden, and create more space for human work.

In a low-trust organization, AI may intensify surveillance, increase fear, and accelerate disengagement.

In a care-centered organization, AI may be used to protect human attention for the moments that matter most.

In an extractive organization, AI may be used to remove people from processes where relationship is essential.

The tool matters.

The system matters more.

## 15. Inquiry Area Five: AI and Care

Care is often absent from AI strategy.

This absence is revealing.

Many forms of care do not appear clearly in process maps, job descriptions, performance dashboards, or productivity metrics.

Care often lives in the informal layer of work and life: the follow-up call, the memory of a person's context, the noticing that something is wrong, the emotional labour of holding uncertainty, the relational repair after conflict, the quiet translation between system and human need.

Because care is often invisible, it is vulnerable to being automated away or intensified without recognition.

AI can support care when it reduces burden, improves access, helps organize information, supports caregivers, or frees human beings from tasks that drain attention away from relationship.

But AI can also erase care when systems confuse responsiveness with relationship, personalization with knowing, or efficiency with dignity.

For example, an AI-generated response to a grieving employee may be grammatically appropriate and emotionally polished. But if no one in the organization has actually made time to care, the message may deepen harm.

The issue is not the tool's wording.

The issue is the absence of relationship behind the wording.

A community organization may use AI to speed up intake summaries. This may reduce administrative burden and help people receive support sooner. But if the tool strips away the details through which workers understand urgency, vulnerability, safety, or trust, the process may become faster while becoming less caring.

This is why care must be treated as a central AI question.

Before automating, leaders and designers should ask what care is currently present in the process, even if it is informal, inefficient, or difficult to measure. They should ask who carries it, who relies on it, and what would happen if it disappeared.

Care is not the opposite of efficiency.

Care is part of what keeps human systems from failing.

## 16. Inquiry Area Six: AI and Power

AI does not enter a neutral world.

It enters systems shaped by capital, labour, governance, colonial histories, racial and gender inequities, data extraction, platform ownership, surveillance infrastructures, environmental costs, and unequal access to technological power.

A relational approach to AI must therefore include power.

The question is not only whether a system works.

It is who it works for, who it works on, who profits, who is exposed, who is excluded, who is represented, who is made invisible, and who carries the risk.

AI can concentrate power when control over data, infrastructure, models, platforms, and deployment sits in the hands of a small number of actors.

AI can also redistribute capacity when people and communities gain access to tools that help them learn, create, organize, and participate more fully.

Both are possible.

This is why relational literacy must be connected to systems literacy.

A person may have a healthy individual relationship with AI while still participating in harmful systemic patterns.

An organization may adopt AI responsibly in one area while ignoring broader effects on labour, privacy, or community trust.

Power is not a separate issue from relationship.

It shapes the terms of relationship.

A mature AI conversation must therefore ask not only what individuals should do, but what systems must be challenged, governed, redesigned, or refused.

## 17. The Refusal Case: When Relational Literacy Says Stop

Every framework must be able to say no.

If relational literacy can only ever counsel more careful use, it is not a discipline. It is a permission slip.

So this section names the harder edge of the practice: the point at which the honest answer to "What relationship are we creating here?" is that this relationship should not be created at all.

Consider a concrete example.

A hospice care organization is under financial and administrative strain. Staff are stretched. Families often wait days for a follow-up after a death. A well-intentioned director proposes using AI to generate personalized condolence messages to bereaved families — drawing on the patient's record to reference the person by name, recall details of their final weeks, and express the organization's sympathy within hours of a death.

The efficiency case is real. Families would hear something sooner. Staff would be relieved of a painful and time-consuming task. The messages would be warm, specific, and consistent.

A purely technical review might approve it. The tool works. A purely procedural review might approve it. No policy is violated and no data leaves the system.

Relational literacy refuses it.

Not because the technology fails, and not because the intention is bad, but because the entire meaning of the act is the human presence it would replace.

A condolence is not information. It is a person choosing to turn toward another person's grief and stay there for a moment. Its value is inseparable from the fact that someone chose to send it. Automating the gesture does not scale the care. It removes the care and keeps the appearance of it.

And the harm is not neutralized by good intentions. A family that later learns the tender message about their mother's final days was generated automatically does not experience efficiency. They experience the discovery that no one was actually there.

This is the refusal case. It lets us name the conditions under which relational literacy returns a stop rather than a caution.

**Refuse when the value of the act is the human presence itself.** Some things mean what they mean only because a person chose to do them. Condolence, apology, forgiveness, witness, blessing, and testimony belong to this category. To automate them is not to deliver them faster. It is to hollow them out while preserving the shell.

**Refuse when the use depends on concealment.** If the interaction only works because the person on the other side believes a human is present when one is not, the relationship is built on deception. Disclosure is the test. If telling the truth about the AI's involvement would break the effect, the effect was the harm.

**Refuse when efficiency is purchased by hiding the erosion of the very thing the work exists to protect.** When AI makes a caring organization look more responsive while making it less caring, the metric improves as the mission fails. Relational literacy names this trade rather than banking it.

**Refuse when no human will actually bear the consequence.** If AI is being used to place distance between a decision and anyone accountable for it, so that when harm occurs there is no person who can answer for it, the tool is laundering responsibility rather than supporting it.

**Refuse when the person affected, if they knew, would experience it as betrayal.** This is the plainest test of all. Imagine the interaction fully disclosed to the one it lands on. If disclosure would produce not indifference but a sense of having been deceived or abandoned, the relationship should not be created.

None of these refusals is anti-AI.

The same organization could use AI to draft internal summaries, organize follow-up schedules, or free staff time precisely so that a human being can make the condolence call. The refusal is not of the tool. It is of one specific relationship the tool was about to be used to fake.

This is what gives the framework teeth.

> **A framework that can never say no cannot be trusted when it says yes.**

Relational literacy is not only a way of using AI more consciously. It is also the capacity to recognize the moments when the most conscious act is to keep the work human, and to say so.

Sometimes the answer to "What relationship are we creating here?" is: none. And naming that is not a failure of the practice. It is the practice working.

## 18. Applying the Framework Across Contexts

Relational literacy becomes useful when it can move across scales.

At the individual level, it helps people ask whether AI is strengthening their agency, voice, creativity, and discernment.

At the educational level, it helps educators design learning environments where AI supports understanding rather than replacing effort.

At the organizational level, it helps leaders examine how AI changes work, culture, trust, care, accountability, and decision-making.

At the societal level, it helps communities examine how AI intersects with power, labour, inequality, knowledge, governance, and collective responsibility.

The same core question travels across all four levels:

> **What relationship are we creating here?**

The answer will not be the same in every context.

That is the point.

Relational literacy is not a universal rulebook.

It is a disciplined way of asking better questions before patterns become invisible.

The following questions are offered as starting points. They are meant to be adapted, not adopted wholesale. Different contexts will require different forms of inquiry, governance, accountability, and care.

---

## 19. Discussion Questions for Individuals

For individuals using AI, the following questions may support reflection:

Why am I turning to AI in this moment?

Am I seeking support, speed, avoidance, validation, certainty, or replacement?

What am I feeling as I use this?

Is this helping me think more clearly?

Do I understand the output well enough to take responsibility for it?

Is my voice still present?

Am I becoming more capable through this use?

What do I need to verify?

What should I not outsource here?

Is this interaction increasing or reducing my agency?

What relationship with AI am I practicing through repeated use?

These questions are not meant to create guilt.

They are meant to create awareness.

---

## 20. Discussion Questions for Educators

For educators, the inquiry may include:

What learning outcome matters most here?

Where could AI support access, practice, or confidence?

Where could AI allow students to bypass necessary learning?

How will students demonstrate understanding?

How will students learn to question and verify AI output?

How will voice and authorship be preserved?

What emotional responses are students carrying around AI use — shame, relief, fear, pressure, confidence, dependency?

What uses of AI are acceptable, required, restricted, or inappropriate?

How can AI become part of learning without becoming a substitute for learning?

The goal is not merely academic integrity.

The goal is intellectual development in an AI-shaped world.

---

## 21. Discussion Questions for Organizations

For organizations, the inquiry may include:

What problem are we trying to solve with AI?

Is the problem technical, relational, cultural, structural, or strategic?

Who will be affected by this use?

Who has been consulted?

What work is being supported, changed, or replaced?

What care is embedded in the current process?

What should remain human?

What governance is required?

What risks are we introducing?

How will accountability be preserved?

How will trust be affected?

What are people feeling but not saying about this adoption?

What kind of organization are we becoming through this adoption?

These questions are not barriers to innovation.

They are conditions for responsible innovation.

---

## 22. Discussion Questions for Communities and Society

For communities and public life, the inquiry may include:

Who has access to AI, and who does not?

Who benefits from AI adoption?

Who bears the risks?

Whose knowledge is represented?

Whose labour is hidden?

How does AI affect public trust?

How does AI shape information environments?

How does AI interact with existing inequities?

What forms of governance and accountability are needed?

What kind of collective future does this technology make more likely?

These questions require public conversation, not only private adoption.

---

## 23. Toward a More Conscious Relationship With AI

The central argument of this paper is not that AI is good or bad.

The argument is that AI is consequential, and our relationship with it deserves conscious attention.

If AI is held primarily through extraction, it will amplify extraction.

If AI is held primarily through fear, it may produce avoidance, panic, and reaction.

If AI is held primarily through worship, it may produce dependency, deference, and loss of discernment.

If AI is held primarily through productivity, it may accelerate work without asking whether the work is worth doing in its current form.

If AI is held through relational literacy, another possibility opens.

For an individual, AI may become a support for reflection rather than a substitute for agency.

For a student, AI may become a learning companion that strengthens understanding rather than bypasses it.

For an organization, AI may reduce administrative burden while protecting human care, judgment, and accountability.

For a community, AI may become an occasion for public conversation about power, access, labour, trust, and the future being created.

That possibility is not guaranteed by the technology.

It depends on the relationship.

The invitation is not to fear AI, trust AI, reject AI, or master AI as quickly as possible.

The invitation is to stop treating the relationship as an afterthought.

Because the question is not only what AI will become.

The question is also what we will become, together with it.

And that remains, in large measure, ours to decide.

## Appendix A: Core Concepts

### Relational Literacy — Broad Definition

Relational literacy is the capacity to notice, understand, evaluate, and intentionally shape the relationships we create with ourselves, other people, communities, tools, technologies, systems, institutions, land, power, and the wider world.

### Relational Literacy in the Context of AI

Relational literacy in the context of AI is the capacity to recognize that every interaction with an AI system is also shaping the human, the work, the organization, and the wider system in which that interaction occurs — and to act with awareness of that shaping.

### Generative Interface

A generative interface is a non-conscious AI system that can synthesize, extend, transform, and produce language, ideas, structures, or artifacts in response to human input.

It does not possess interiority, selfhood, moral agency, memory in the human sense, or a stake in the outcome.

Yet it can still be consequential.

The term helps hold a middle position between treating AI as a self and dismissing it as nothing. A generative interface may shape thought, language, learning, creativity, work, decision-making, and meaning-making, while remaining without consciousness, intention, or responsibility.

Relational literacy asks humans to remain aware of how they are being shaped in relationship with generative interfaces, and to preserve human agency, care, judgment, authorship, and accountability within that relationship.

### Core Practice Question

> **What relationship are we creating here?**

## Appendix B: Relational Patterns With AI

The following patterns are offered as starting points for discussion, not as fixed categories.

### Extractive Use

AI is used to increase output, reduce cost, or accelerate production without sufficient attention to human impact, care, labour, or consequence.

### Dependent Use

AI becomes a substitute for human judgment, confidence, creativity, authorship, or responsibility.

### Performative Use

AI is used to create the appearance of thought, care, productivity, expertise, or transformation without the substance behind it.

### Transactional Use

AI is used for bounded tasks with appropriate human oversight and limited relational consequence.

### Reflective Use

AI is used to support thinking, learning, perspective-taking, synthesis, and discernment.

### Creative Use

AI is used to help bring ideas into form while preserving human authorship, intention, voice, and responsibility.

### Stewardship Use

AI is used with explicit attention to care, governance, consequence, accountability, power, and human agency.

For any AI use, ask:
